02 — Overview

Deal description

Spruce Place is a 32-unit scattered-site townhome portfolio in Lakeville, Minnesota — 93.75% occupied, managed by incumbent third-party PM, and producing $481K of Year 1 NOI against a $8.5M basis. Twenty-six of thirty-two units were built in 2017–2018; none are older than 2005. This is stabilized stock, not a turnaround.

The return composition tells the story. Over ten years, 35% of total return comes from annual cash distributions, 20% from the depreciation tax shield, 16% from principal paydown, and 29% from market appreciation. Fifty-five percent of the return is realized during the hold — not at sale. Partners earn while they wait.

Each of the 32 units has its own parcel ID, which gives the portfolio a dual-track exit option: whole-portfolio sale to an investor or individual retail disposition at $280–$325K per unit (approximately 5–22% uplift over our $266K per-unit acquisition basis). We're not modeling the retail path — it's optionality, not thesis.

Hold philosophy
Our exit philosophy is to not exit. We build long-term portfolios that produce recurring monthly income into the future. Our preferred path is to refinance at Year 5 and Year 10, pull cash out tax-free where the rate environment permits, and keep operating the asset at a consistent cashflow. 06 — Refinance discipline

Refinance what-if analysis

Refinance what-if analysis — self-contained. The slider below tests cash-out scenarios at different refi rates and does not cascade to any other section's tables or metrics.

Our original loan ends at Year 5 — we must refinance at whatever rate exists then. From the Y5 balance, we calculate the break-even rate: the rate at which we could borrow the remaining balance at our original annual payment over a fresh 30 years. That's our pivot. Below break-even: we refinance into a larger loan, cash out the difference to partners, payment stays the same. Above break-even: we keep the same balance, but our annual payment rises — operations must absorb the increase.

09 — What could go wrong

Risks & constraints

A constraint to note.

This deal fails one of our structure-dependent hurdles: Year 1 DSCR of 1.244 (our target is 1.25 — off by 6 bps). Noted.

The structure-independent test — going-in cap rate of 5.66% vs. our 5.0% floor — passes with 66 bps of margin.

Read this carefully. If a tight DSCR in the conservative case disqualifies the deal for you, that's a reasonable read. Let us know.

Year 5 refinance rate risk
Our 5.75% loan ends at Y5. The refi break-even rate is 6.32% — we have 57 bps of cushion against today's 5.75% market. If rates spike above 6.32% by Y5 (2031), our annual payment rises and operations must absorb the increase. The deal doesn't break at modest rate spikes (DSCR stays above 1.20 through a ~7% refi rate), but rising rates compress coverage and need to be watched.
Insurance & expense pressure
MN insurance has hardened 5–10%/yr since 2022. Model uses 5%. Harder trajectory compresses NOI growth.
Exit cap softening
If rates stay elevated through 2035, exit cap could widen from 5.0% toward 6.0–6.5%. Scenario B captures most of this; deeper widening exists as tail risk.
Occupancy regression
Current 93.75%, two units vacant. Model assumes 5% vacancy. Regional recession could push to 88–90% temporarily.
